\subsection{Introduction}\label{introduction}

\begin{quote}
Orbital cellulitis (OC) is a serious infection of the orbital tissues that can lead to vision-threatening and life-threatening complications, often requiring hospitalization and, in severe cases, surgical intervention. Clinical presentations can vary, ranging from localized inflammation to the development of orbital and intracranial complications such as subperiosteal abscess (SPA), orbital abscess (OA), and intracranial extension (ICE).

The data for these analyses consist of hospital admissions for OC from June 2006 through May 2025, derived from a retrospective chart review across three hospital systems: Denver Health (DH), University of Colorado (UC), and Oregon Health and Science University (OHSU). The dataset includes 330 adult subjects with detailed clinical, demographic, and outcome information collected at the time of admission.

The primary objectives of this analysis were to identify factors associated with need for surgical intervention and length of hospital stay (LOS) among adults admitted for orbital cellulitis. Secondary analyses examined factors associated with the development of complications, including SPA, OA, and ICE. We hypothesized that markers of disease severity and underlying comorbidities would be associated with increased likelihood of surgery and prolonged LOS.
\end{quote}

\subsection{Methods}\label{methods}

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  OR - relates to probability of an event
\item
  RR - relates to amount of something (counts/time)

  \begin{itemize}
  \tightlist
  \item
    OR=2 ≠ RR=2 - OR is usually more extreme when event is more common
  \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

Logistic regression models estimated odds ratios (ORs) for binary outcomes,
representing the relative odds of an event. Negative binomial models estimated
rate ratios (RRs) for length of stay, representing multiplicative changes in
expected LOS.

\textbf{Dacryocystitis} is an infection of the tear sac (lacrimal sac) caused by a
blockage in the tear duct (nasolacrimal duct), leading to pain, redness, and

Neutrophils = innate, acute inflammation
Lymphocytes (T,B,NK) = adaptive, regulated immunity
* Neutrophils ↑ = acute inflammation, stress, infection
* Lymphocytes ↓ = stress response, immune suppression, chronic disease
* So high NLR = More inflammation + relatively weaker adaptive immune response

Physiological meaning of high vs low NLR

High NLR:
* Acute stress or infection
* Chronic inflammation
* Poor prognosis in some diseases

Low NLR:
* Lower inflammation
* Stronger relative adaptive immunity (context-dependent)

\subparagraph{GLMnet}\label{glmnet}

\url{https://stackoverflow.com/questions/12937331/why-is-it-inadvisable-to-get-statistical-summary-information-for-regression-coef/17725220\#17725220}

``It is a very natural question to ask for standard errors of regression
coefficients or other estimated quantities. In principle such standard errors
can easily be calculated, e.g.~using the bootstrap.

Still, this package deliberately does not provide them. The reason for this is
that standard errors are not very meaningful for strongly biased estimates such
as arise from penalized estimation methods. Penalized estimation is a procedure
that reduces the variance of estimators by introducing substantial bias. The
bias of each estimator is therefore a major component of its mean squared error,
whereas its variance may contribute only a small part.

Unfortunately, in most applications of penalized regression it is impossible to
obtain a sufficiently precise estimate of the bias. Any bootstrap-based
calculations can only give an assessment of the variance of the estimates.
Reliable estimates of the bias are only available if reliable unbiased estimates
are available, which is typically not the case in situations in which penalized
estimates are used.

Reporting a standard error of a penalized estimate therefore tells only part of
the story. It can give a mistaken impression of great precision, completely
ignoring the inaccuracy caused by the bias. It is certainly a mistake to make
confidence statements that are only based on an assessment of the variance of
the estimates, such as bootstrap-based confidence intervals do.''
